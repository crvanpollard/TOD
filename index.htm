Evaluating Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) Opportunities in Greater Philadelphia

Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) is compact, mixed-use development within an easy walk of a transit station. Its pedestrian-oriented design encourages residents and workers to drive their cars less and ride mass transit more.

TOD has played an essential role in the historical growth and development of the Greater Philadelphia region. Today, TOD remains an integral part of a centers-based strategy for regional growth because it can enhance access to jobs, promote healthy and active lifestyles, and create vibrant communities with a variety of transportation choices.

This web application and the associated report are DVRPCâ€™s latest effort to provide a systematic approach to assessing TOD investment opportunities throughout the region*. It is designed to provide public officials, planners, transit operators, developers, and citizens with resources that can help guide public and private investment at rail stations.

Stations located within an area largely corresponding to Center City Philadelphia were not included in this analysis because the TOD potential of these station areas has been well established. Methodology

Station areas that possess a favorable combination of transit service, real estate conditions, developable property, and local government support represent potential TOD opportunities.

DVRPC used 12 individual attributes divided into two complementary categories to evaluate the TOD readiness of station areas throughout the region. These categories describe separate yet related aspects of each station area. Composite scores for each category were generated by averaging the scores of the individual attributes within each category. Each station area was assigned a pair of scores which together can be used to evaluate its overall TOD readiness.

Data Analysis

This evaluation of TOD readiness uses 12 factors divided into two categories. To create an easily understood rating system, each factor was rated on a scale of one to four with four being the highest rating. In some cases, this ranking was based on a quartile distribution of that factor across all stations. In other cases, the TOD ratings were based on predetermined scoring ranges that help to differentiate station areas. Details on the specific evaluation criteria can be found on the map portion of this web site.

The two categories used in this analysis describe separate yet related aspects of each station areaâ€™s readiness for TOD. The first category, Existing TOD Orientation, describes how supportive the existing conditions of a station area are of the land use and travel patterns that are frequently associated with TOD. The second category, Future TOD Potential, inventories data related to real estate market conditions and transit supportive planning efforts that are suggestive of future development opportunities. Composite scores for each category were generated by averaging the scores of individual attributes within each category.

It is important to note that these scores represent a generalized assessment of TOD opportunities in our region. The rating system was designed to identify individual strengths and weaknesses within a station area (generally one-half mile) while also allowing for comparisons between station areas in different parts of the region. Lower ratings should not discourage proactive planning efforts designed to make a station area more vibrant, accessible, or attractive. Instead, lower scores can help guide the efforts of those wishing to encourage TOD in a specific location.

The charts on this page plots each station area based on the pair of scores generated by this analysis. The dashed lines represent the median scores in each category for all stations being evaluated. Stations falling within Quadrant Two may represent the strongest TOD opportunities because they score well in both categories when compared to other stations throughout the region. Transit Service Quality

The quality of transit service is measured using the Transit Connectivity Index (TCI) developed by the Center for Neighborhood Technology (CNT). A TCI score reflects the number of transit trips that are accessible by walking in a specific location. This measure examines the frequency and proximity of all transit routes and so this score also reflects nearby bus service.

Travel Time

Transit service is most attractive when travel times are competitive with driving times. This attribute is based on a ratio of transit to automobile travel time from a station area to Suburban Station in Center City Philadelphia, a central location within the regionâ€™s largest employment center. This ratio was derived using travel times from Google Maps based on a 9:00 AM arrival on a Tuesday morning. For transit trips, transfer and walking times are included. Travel time ratios of 1.0 or more reflect trips that are quicker by transit during this time period.
