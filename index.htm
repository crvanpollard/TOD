Existing TOD Orientatio Category Description

The seven attributes in this category provide an overview of the existing transit, demographics, and physical context of a station area. Together, these attributes help to identify station areas where the land use and travel patterns frequently associated with TOD already exist.

  • Transit Service Quality
  • Job Access
  • Travel Time
  • Intensity
  • Car Ownership
  • Non-Car Commuters
  • Walk Score®

Transit Service Quality

The quality of transit service is measured using the Transit Connectivity Index (TCI) developed by the Center for Neighborhood Technology (CNT). A TCI score reflects the number of transit trips that are accessible by walking in a specific location. This measure examines the frequency and proximity of all transit routes and so this score also reflects nearby bus service.

Job Access

The number of jobs accessible by transit directly influences how desirable a station area is to potential residents and businesses. This attribute identifies the number of jobs accessible via a 30-minute transit ride from a given station. This data was aggregated by CNT as part of their Housing and Transportation (H+T) Affordability Index.

Scoring

Higher scores are more supportive of TOD

Travel Time

Transit service is most attractive when travel times are competitive with driving times. This attribute is based on a ratio of transit to automobile travel time from a station area to Suburban Station in Center City Philadelphia, a central location within the region’s largest employment center. This ratio was derived using travel times from Google Maps based on a 9:00 AM arrival on a Tuesday morning. For transit trips, transfer and walking times are included. Travel time ratios of 1.0 or more reflect trips that are quicker by transit during this time period.


Scoring

Higher scores are more supportive of TOD
4 = 1.0 and greater
3 = 0.75—0.99
2 = 0.50—0.74
1 = 0.49 and below

Intensity

These projects include needs for miscellaneous items, such as parking facilities, drainage, environmental mitigation, Transportation Management Associations (TMAs), engineering, regional and local planning, and debt service. These needs are based on forecasting future need based on projects and costs that are included in the current Transportation Improvement Programs (TIP) for Pennsylvania and New Jersey.

Car Ownership

These projects represent needs for existing rail infrastructure, vehicle fleets, and stations. Regular vehicle track, signal, catenary, power substations, signals, vehicle overhaul and replacement, station renovations, and ADA accessibility needs were used to develop the need for each of these three categories using asset management data.

Non-Car Commuters

These projects aim to improve the functionality of the existing system. Types of projects include real-time information systems, signal preemption, fare modernization, and double tracking and sidings to improve service frequency. The estimated needs were developed by DVRPC and regional transit agencies.


Scoring

Walk Score®

These projects include new transit facilities, routes, and lines that the region would like to pursue. Need for this category is based on a short list of projects developed by the long-range plan subcommittee and includes projects listed in the Connections 2035 Plan and DVRPC’s Long-Range Vision for Transit Report.
